About

A research lab for evidence custody in automated systems.

What this is

Modern automation tends to act on claims: a credential is valid, a monitor is green, a model says tests passed, a policy engine allowed it, a human approved. The hard part is preserving whether those claims were witnessed, still fresh, and admissible at the moment of action. The instruments here exist to keep that gap visible and refusable — small pieces that separate observation from assertion, measure the gap between check and action on a named clock, gate intent before it becomes effect, and emit receipts the next system over can act on.

Authorization is not custody

Authorization asks whether an action is allowed. Custody asks whether the premises behind that action are still good enough to act on. A credential valid at check time can be stale at spend time. A green check can outlive its evidence. A log line is self-report until sealed. A demonstrated behavior is not an operational claim. Each of those collapses is invisible to the conventional stack and unrecoverable after the fact. Keeping them visible — and refusable — is the whole job.

Where to start

Different readers want different doors.

  • Operations / SREThe incident write-up — check-then-act race condition, naive auth vs custody, in postmortem form. Then nq for the observation layer.
  • AI agentsagent_governor and wicket — typed proposals in, gate verdicts and refusal receipts out.
  • Formal methodsThe Lean proof reader’s portal, where class boundaries of refusals are machine-checked.
  • AT ProtocolThe labelwatch / driftwatch / receipts tooling. Treat it as a field lab where custody ideas meet a live network, not as a peer of the core stack.
  • Cold readerStart with the incident if you read postmortems; the descent if you want the artifact-rich version. Both are the same event.

What this is not

  • Not an observability dashboard. Dashboards display claims after the fact; custody decides whether a claim may become a premise in the first place.
  • Not a policy-as-code replacement. Policy engines decide over premises; custody decides whether the premises are admissible. The two compose.
  • Not “AI governance” in the procurement sense. Refusals and receipts you can run.
  • Not a hosted service or a production platform. Alpha, solo, small on purpose.
  • Not a proof of safety. Lean checks refusal-class boundaries. Receipts attribute instance facts. Both are narrower than “proven” — see limits.

Why a lab and not a product

The bet is that the right unit of governance for automated systems is the conversion between observation, claim, premise, authority, action, and receipt — and that ordinary infrastructure collapses those boundaries into “looks fine” in ways that only show up at incident time. A bet like that wants to be falsifiable, so the work ships as small instruments with explicit refusal boundaries and reproducible demos.
